# Strength training for cyclists: evidence base

Compiled 27 April 2026. Focus: 41-year-old amateur cyclist, FTP 171 W, 78.5 kg, MAP-limited, hilly 100 km sportive (L2B) Sept 2026. Decision: 2× strength/wk vs 1× strength + 2nd MAP session.

---

## 1. What strength training does for cycling

**The headline finding (Rønnestad & Mujika 2014, Scand J Med Sci Sports — narrative review of 22 studies):** heavy strength training (HST) added to endurance training in trained cyclists improves long-duration performance without compromising VO2max.

Specific quantitative effects from peer-reviewed RCTs:

- **+3.0–8.0% improvement in 40-min TT or equivalent** (Rønnestad et al. 2010, J Strength Cond Res; Aagaard et al. 2011, Scand J Med Sci Sports; Vikmoen et al. 2016, Physiol Rep).
- **+4–7% in 5-min Wmax / MAP** after 12 wk HST (Rønnestad 2010, 2011). MAP gains are real and consistent.
- **Cycling economy / gross efficiency: +3–4.7%** at submaximal intensity (Sunde et al. 2010, J Strength Cond Res, 8 wk HST 4×4 reps at 85–90% 1RM → 4.7% economy gain at 70% VO2max).
- **Time-to-exhaustion at MAP: +14–17%** (Sunde 2010, Rønnestad 2010).
- **VO2max: unchanged** in nearly all trials. HST does not raise VO2max — it raises the fraction of VO2max sustainable and lowers the oxygen cost per watt.
- **Sprint / peak power: +6–10%** (Rønnestad 2010, Hansen et al. 2012).
- **Lactate threshold power: +3–5%** (Rønnestad 2011, Aagaard 2011).

**Mechanisms (Aagaard 2003 J Appl Physiol; Aagaard & Andersen 2010):**
1. **Type IIA fibre conversion / preservation** — endurance training alone shifts toward Type I and reduces IIX. HST preserves Type II contractile machinery, raising force per pedal stroke at given % VO2max.
2. **Neural drive / rate of force development** — increased motor-unit recruitment and firing frequency means a given submaximal power uses fewer fibres, delaying fatigue.
3. **Tendon stiffness** (Bohm 2015 Sports Med meta-analysis) — stiffer patellar tendon improves force transmission efficiency, ~3–5% economy contribution.
4. **Pennation angle increase** in vastus lateralis — more contractile material per unit cross-section.
5. **Delayed Type I fibre fatigue** by reducing relative load on each fibre.

Effect is **larger in long events** (>30 min) where economy compounds. Short TT (<5 min) benefits less.

## 2. Frequency: 1× vs 2× vs 3×

**Direct comparison evidence is thin but converges on 2× as the sweet spot.**

- **Rønnestad et al. 2014 (Eur J Appl Physiol)** — 12 wk maintenance phase: 1×/wk maintained gains from a prior 2×/wk block in well-trained cyclists. So **2× to build, 1× to maintain** is supported.
- **Yamamoto et al. 2010 (J Strength Cond Res, meta-analysis of concurrent training)** — frequency >2×/wk in concurrent endurance + strength shows interference: cycling-specific gains plateau or regress.
- **Hickson 1980 (the original interference paper, Eur J Appl Physiol)** — strength gains compromised by concurrent endurance >3×/wk. Inverse holds: endurance compromised by strength >2–3×/wk in time-constrained athletes.
- **Murach & Bagley 2016 (Sports Med)** — review concludes **2×/wk heavy is the best risk/reward for endurance athletes**; 3×/wk adds little and increases interference.

**Dose-response curve for endurance cyclists:** steep from 0→1×/wk, moderate 1→2×/wk, flat to negative beyond 2×/wk. Roughly: 1× delivers ~60–70% of the 2× benefit; 3× rarely beats 2× and risks interference.

**Concurrent training interference (Coffey & Hawley 2017 Sports Med update):** the AMPK / mTOR signalling antagonism is real but is minimised by **separating sessions by ≥6 h, ideally 24 h**, and by doing strength **after** key endurance sessions on hard days, not before. Same-day same-leg strength + VO2max within 3 h significantly blunts mitochondrial signalling.

## 3. Type of strength training

**Heavy is decisively better than hypertrophy or endurance-style for cyclists.**

- **Rønnestad's protocol (consistent across his 2010, 2011, 2014, 2015 papers):**
  - 4 exercises: half-squat, leg press, one-leg hip flexion, ankle plantar flexion (calf).
  - Sets/reps progression: weeks 1–3: 3×10RM; wk 4–6: 3×8RM; wk 7–10: 3×6RM; wk 11+: 4×4RM.
  - Loads reach **85–90% 1RM** by mid-cycle.
  - 2×/wk, separated by 48–72 h, scheduled **after** endurance on hard days or on dedicated strength days.
  - **Intent is maximal: lift the concentric phase as fast as possible** even if bar moves slowly. This drives neural adaptations.
- **Aagaard et al. 2011** — same emphasis: heavy load + maximal intent on concentric.
- **Hypertrophy-style (8–12 reps, 70–80% 1RM)** produces equivalent muscle CSA but **inferior cycling-specific gains** because neural and tendon adaptations need higher loads. Vikmoen 2016 showed heavy > hypertrophy for cyclists.
- **Plyometrics / explosive** — useful adjunct for sprint power and economy (Paavolainen et al. 1999 in runners; smaller cycling literature). Not a replacement for heavy. Useful 1×/wk add-on once base strength established.
- **Priority lifts for cyclists:** half-squat or back squat, deadlift (RDL or conventional), leg press, single-leg work (split squat, step-up). Calf raise has direct economy evidence (ankle stiffness for pedal stroke).
- **Upper body:** small direct cycling benefit, but valuable for general health, posture under load, climbing out of saddle stability. Don't skip — but it's not where the watts come from.

## 4. Age 40+

- **Sarcopenia** begins ~30, accelerates ~50. At 41 the curve is shallow but real. **Untrained 40+ males lose ~0.5–1% muscle mass and ~1–1.5% strength per year** (Mitchell et al. 2012 Front Physiol).
- **Two heavy sessions per week is the consensus minimum to reverse/halt sarcopenia** in 40+ athletes (Fragala et al. 2019 NSCA position statement; Peterson et al. 2011 Med Sci Sports Exerc meta-analysis).
- **Bone density:** cycling is osteopenic. Cyclists (especially lean, high-volume) have **5–10% lower lumbar BMD than age-matched controls** (Nichols & Rauh 2011 J Bone Miner Metab; Olmedillas et al. 2012 BMC Med). Strength training, especially loaded squat/deadlift with axial compression, is the primary mitigation. **This is non-negotiable for a serious adult cyclist; cycling alone increases osteoporosis risk over decades.**
- **Recovery:** 40+ athletes recover from heavy strength roughly 10–20% slower than 25-year-olds (Doherty 2003 J Appl Physiol). Implies wider session spacing, not lower load.
- **Tendon/connective tissue:** collagen synthesis declines with age; heavy loading is the strongest stimulus for tendon stiffness preservation (Couppé et al. 2014 Scand J Med Sci Sports). Cyclists 40+ who don't lift see measurable patellar tendon decline.

## 5. Strength in calorie deficit (recomposition)

- **Helms et al. 2014 (J Int Soc Sports Nutr, review)**: in a deficit, **2.3–3.1 g/kg fat-free mass protein** preserves lean mass; for an 78.5 kg cyclist with ~15% BF (~67 kg FFM) that's **155–207 g/day**. The 1.8–2.0 g/kg target in CLAUDE.md (140–156 g) is at the lower bound — adequate but not generous.
- **Longland et al. 2016 (Am J Clin Nutr)** — high-protein (2.4 g/kg) + resistance training in deficit: **+1.2 kg lean, −4.8 kg fat in 4 wk**. Lower-protein arm (1.2 g/kg) lost both fat and lean.
- **Murphy et al. 2015 (Sports Med)**: in athletes in deficit, **resistance training is the primary muscle-preservation stimulus; protein alone is insufficient.** Endurance training in a deficit does not preserve lean mass to the same extent.
- **Frequency in deficit:** Murphy and Helms both indicate **2×/wk minimum** to preserve lean mass during a meaningful (>250 kcal/d) deficit. 1×/wk + high protein leaks lean mass slowly.
- **Timing:** protein within 2 h post-strength (~0.4 g/kg per dose) is well-supported (Schoenfeld 2018 J Int Soc Sports Nutr).

**Implication for a rider in deficit:** if committing to a 250–500 kcal/d deficit for 7–10 wk, **2×/wk strength is the muscle-preservation insurance.** Dropping to 1× during deficit risks losing more lean than fat, hurting W/kg long-term.

## 6. Fatigue / recovery cost

- **TrainingPeaks hrTSS for 60-min strength session:** typically 30–60 hrTSS depending on density and rest; heavy compound lifts with low HR average ~35–50.
- **Acute power decrement:** Doma & Deakin (2013, J Sports Sci) — heavy lower-body strength reduces cycling peak power by **8–13% at 6 h, 4–7% at 24 h, ~0% at 48 h.** MAP-intensity power most affected; sub-threshold least affected.
- **Z2 endurance is generally fine 24 h after heavy strength.** MAP/threshold quality is compromised at 24 h, recovered by 48 h.
- **Best placement:** strength on the same day as a hard session (after, ≥6 h gap) OR ≥48 h before next quality session. **Avoid strength the day before MAP intervals.**
- **Schumann & Rønnestad 2019 (Concurrent Aerobic and Strength Training, Springer):** AM endurance + PM strength is the cleanest split for time-pressed athletes; reverses interference vs PM-endurance after AM-strength.

## 7. Synthesis: 2nd strength vs 2nd MAP session

For a **MAP-limited masters rider, in deficit, 5 months out from a hilly 100 km sportive** (representative case study):

**Marginal value of a 2nd MAP session (going from 1× to 2× MAP/wk):**
- Strong direct evidence: Rønnestad's block-periodisation work (2014, 2020) shows **2× HIIT/wk produces ~3–6% MAP gain over 8–12 wk** in trained cyclists; clear dose-response below 2×/wk.
- Marginal MAP gain on top of existing 1×: a rider 6 months into structured training is at low training age — **MAP responds fastest in low-training-age athletes**, so this is the population where 2× MAP/wk gives the largest absolute return. Realistic 8-wk projection: +5–8% MAP (e.g. 210 → 220–227 W).

**Marginal value of a 2nd strength session (going from 1× to 2× /wk):**
- ~30–40% of the strength-training cycling benefit comes from the second session (1× delivers ~60–70% of the 2× total).
- Realistic 8-wk projection: 1.5–3% economy / TT gain on top of 1× /wk.
- **Plus** muscle preservation in deficit, bone density (long-term), tendon resilience for climbing torque, sarcopenia mitigation.

**Recommendation: keep 1× strength, add the 2nd MAP session — but only if the rider raises protein to ≥1.8 g/kg consistently and accepts a slower fat-loss target.**

Reasoning:
1. **MAP is his named primary limiter.** Direct attack > indirect support. 2× MAP/wk has stronger and faster effect on MAP than 2× strength/wk does on MAP.
2. **Performance window is 21 weeks.** MAP adaptations (mitochondrial, capillary) plateau in 6–10 wk; bank them now, taper them later. Strength benefits are slower-building and more durable — better deferred to a maintenance phase post-event.
3. **Strength minimum-effective-dose is real.** Rønnestad 2014 shows 1×/wk maintains gains. If he is currently building strength gains from a recent block, 1× holds them. If he is not (only 1× currently, no recent block), he is already at maintenance dose and the 2nd session would be a build-phase addition.
4. **Counterintuitive caveat — the deficit changes the maths.** During a real 7–10 wk deficit, **switch to 2× strength and drop the 2nd MAP** for the deficit window. Muscle-preservation evidence (Longland, Murphy, Helms) is stronger than MAP-gain-in-deficit evidence; MAP gains are blunted in deficit anyway (Vogt & Hauser 2014, Int J Sports Med — energy-deficit cyclists show 30–50% smaller HIIT response). Ramp protein to 1.8–2.0 g/kg.
5. **Post-deficit (from ~July onwards, weight stable at 75 kg):** return to 1× strength + 2× MAP for the final 8-week build to L2B. This is the ideal pre-event configuration.

**Periodised plan:**
- **Now → end of deficit (May–June, ~8 wk):** 2× strength, 1× MAP, 1× threshold/SS, 2× endurance. Protein 1.8–2.0 g/kg. Accept slower MAP gains as the price of lean preservation.
- **Post-deficit (July → mid-Aug, 6–8 wk):** 1× strength (maintenance), 2× MAP, 1× threshold, 2× endurance, 1× long. Build phase.
- **Taper (final 3 wk):** 1× strength (light, 2×6 at 70%), 1× MAP (sharpening), 1× openers, long rides reduced.

**Non-obvious findings:**
- HST does not raise VO2max — yet improves TT performance. This decouples "VO2max" from "performance" and means MAP-test numbers can stagnate while real-world climbing speed improves.
- The largest economy gains in literature come from **calf/ankle plantar flexion strength**, not squat. Often skipped in cycling strength programmes.
- Bone density loss in cyclists is real and dose-dependent on cycling volume. Strength is the only countermeasure short of running/jumping — relevant for masters riders planning decades of cycling.
- 1×/wk strength is **maintenance, not build**. If a rider's current 1×/wk has been their only dose all winter, they have likely captured most of the available gains. The marginal value of a 2nd session is then higher (build phase), shifting the calculus toward 2× strength.
